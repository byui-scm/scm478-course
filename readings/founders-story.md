# Founder's Story: Peak Fuel Foods

*Read before Week 1, Day 1.*

## How It Started

My name is Maria Solano, and I started Peak Fuel Foods out of my kitchen in Logan, Utah about three years ago. The idea was simple: I was tired of the protein bars and meal-prep options on the market being either tasteless or full of ingredients I couldn't pronounce. I'd been making protein burritos for my climbing partners and people kept asking where they could buy them. So I figured I'd try selling them.

The first year was just me and a commercial kitchen I rented by the hour. I made protein burritos — that was it. One product, one size. I sold them at farmers markets, dropped them off at a couple of local gyms, and took orders through Instagram DMs. I kept track of everything in a notebook and a couple of Google Sheets.

Things moved fast in year two. A friend connected me to a buyer at a regional grocery chain, and suddenly I had real purchase orders for the first time. I added four more products: a protein bowl, a breakfast wrap, a snack bar, and a smoothie pack. I moved into a shared commercial kitchen with more capacity, hired two part-time people, and started working with suppliers instead of just buying ingredients at Costco.

Now, in year three, we're in about 40 stores across Utah, Idaho, and Wyoming — mostly regional chains and natural food stores, but we just got a test placement in 12 Target locations and we're in conversation with Kroger. Revenue last year was about $480K. This year we're projecting $650K, maybe $700K if the Target test goes well.

---

## What We Sell

We have five products right now:

**The Original Protein Burrito** is still our best seller. Seasoned chicken, black beans, brown rice, roasted peppers, cheese, and a chipotle sauce, all wrapped in a flour tortilla. Each one has 32 grams of protein. We make about 200 a week right now, sometimes more when a retailer runs a promotion.

**The Protein Power Bowl** is the same filling concept but in a microwaveable bowl instead of a tortilla — no wrap, add quinoa. It's our fastest-growing product. We're making about 100 a week.

**The Sunrise Breakfast Wrap** is scrambled egg, turkey sausage, sweet potato, and pepper jack cheese in a whole wheat tortilla. It was designed for the morning occasion. Maybe 120 a week.

**The Peak Bar** is our snack play — a baked protein bar with oats, peanut butter, whey protein, dark chocolate chips, and honey. We make these in batches of 500 or so, and they move about 130 a week across all channels. They have the longest shelf life of anything we make.

**The Green Fuel Smoothie Pack** is frozen — spinach, banana, protein powder, almond butter, and almond milk, pre-portioned so you just blend and go. This one's the trickiest from a supply chain perspective because it has to stay frozen all the way to the store. We're at about 50 a week and honestly I'm not sure if the margins work yet.

## How We Make It

Every product has a recipe card — I call them recipe cards but really they're just the ingredient lists I've built up over time. Each recipe specifies exactly how much of each ingredient goes into one unit of each product. For example, one Protein Burrito uses about 6 ounces of seasoned chicken, 3 ounces of black beans, 2 ounces of brown rice, and so on.

Some ingredients are shared across products — chicken is in the burritos and the bowls, cheese shows up in three products, the protein powder is in both the bars and the smoothie packs.

Not every ingredient converts perfectly. When you dice onions, you lose some. When you cook rice, the yield isn't exactly what you measured dry. I've figured out yield factors for most ingredients through trial and error — for example, if I start with 100 pounds of raw chicken breast, I'll get about 85 pounds of usable cooked chicken after trimming, cooking, and cooling. Those yield numbers are in my head and on sticky notes, but I should probably have them written down somewhere more permanent.

We produce Monday through Thursday. Friday is for cleaning, inventory counts, and placing orders for the next week. It's a fixed weekly cycle — I look at what we need to make next week, figure out what ingredients I need, check what's on hand, and order the difference.

## Our Suppliers

We work with about a dozen suppliers now. Some are local — there's a poultry processor in Tremonton that supplies our chicken, and a local tortilla company for the wraps. Others are regional distributors. A few specialty ingredients (the specific whey protein isolate we use, the chipotle sauce base) come from national suppliers.

Each supplier has different terms. Lead times range from next-day for local produce to three weeks for specialty items. Minimum order quantities vary — the tortilla company has a 20-case minimum, the protein powder supplier requires 12 bags per order. Some suppliers give us net-30 payment terms, others want payment on delivery.

I have a Google Sheet with all the supplier contact information and terms, but I'll be honest — it's not always up to date. When a supplier changes their MOQ or adjusts their lead time, I usually find out when I try to place an order and they tell me.

## Where the Data Lives

Our "system" is basically a collection of Google Sheets and some email folders. Here's what we have:

**Products & Pricing** — every SKU, the product name, descriptions, and what we charge retailers. I update it whenever we change pricing, which has been twice this year.

**Recipes & Ingredients** — every ingredient in every product, how much goes into each unit. I also have notes about yields and suppliers in there, though it's gotten kind of messy because I keep adding columns.

**Sales Log** — retail orders come as POs via email; direct-to-consumer orders come through our website and I export those. The format isn't always consistent because retail POs look different from Shopify exports.

**Inventory Count** — I fill this in every Friday. I walk through the kitchen, count everything, and type the numbers in. It has the ingredient, the quantity on hand, the date, and sometimes notes like "looks low" or "opened package."

**PO Tracker** — when I order from a supplier, I log the PO number, what I ordered, how much, when I ordered it, when it's expected, and when it actually shows up. The "when it actually shows up" column has a lot of blanks because I forget to update it when things arrive.

**Receiving Log** — when shipments come in, I'm supposed to log what showed up, how much, and any quality issues. I'm about 60% consistent on this. When there's a problem, I definitely write it down. When everything's fine, I sometimes skip it.

I know I should also be tracking things like lot numbers for traceability, cost trends over time, supplier performance metrics, and detailed production records. A real ERP system would do all of this automatically. But a real ERP system would also cost more than my first year's revenue, so here we are.

## What Keeps Me Up at Night

Every Monday morning I need to know: what do we need to make this week, do we have the ingredients, and if not, what do I need to order and from whom? That sounds simple but it involves looking at four or five sheets, doing mental math on the recipes, and comparing against inventory. It takes me about two hours every Monday and I'm never 100% sure I haven't missed something.

The fire drills are less predictable. Last month, our chicken supplier had a processing line go down and couldn't fill our order for two weeks. I had to figure out which products were affected, how much chicken I had on hand, whether I could get chicken from another supplier (I could, but at 15% higher cost and a different cut), and which customer orders were at risk. That took me most of a day to work through, and I'm still not sure I got it right.

The Target test placement is both exciting and terrifying. They want consistent supply, accurate lead times, and the ability to trace any product back to its ingredient lots if there's ever a recall. I can barely trace what I ordered last week, let alone connect a specific burrito to the lot of chicken that went into it.

If we get the full Kroger placement, the volume will roughly double what we're doing now. I genuinely don't know if our current "system" can handle that.

## The Numbers

For reference, here are the rough operational parameters of the business:

- Five products, approximately 33 ingredients across all recipes
- Some ingredients are in multiple products (chicken, cheese, onions); others are product-specific (tortillas, smoothie cups)
- Weekly production typically ranges from 50 to 200 units per product
- Demand has been growing about 4–5% month over month, with seasonal bumps in January and softness in summer
- 12 suppliers; lead times range from 1 to 3 weeks; most have minimum order quantities
- Costs range from $0.50/unit for basic ingredients to $20+ for specialty items like protein powder
- Fixed weekly ordering cycle: orders placed Friday for delivery the following week or week after

Current inventory on hand varies wildly. Some things I have too much of (I over-ordered tortillas last month and we're swimming in them), some things I'm always running low on (the chipotle sauce seems to disappear faster than my recipe says it should — probably a yield problem I haven't nailed down).

---

If someone could take these sheets and make them actually talk to each other — so that on Monday morning I could just ask "what do I need to order?" and get a reliable answer — that would change my life. And if that system could also help me figure out what's going wrong when a supplier is late or when demand spikes unexpectedly, even better.

I don't need it to be perfect. I need it to be better than what I'm doing now, which is basically running a half-million dollar company out of my head and a handful of spreadsheets.
