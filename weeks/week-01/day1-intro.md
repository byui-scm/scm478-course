## Why This Class Will Change How You Work

Most supply chain courses teach you how supply chains work. This course teaches you how to **build tools that run them**.

Here is what that difference means in practice:

### You will speak two languages

Every company has people who understand the operations (buyers, planners, warehouse managers) and people who build the systems (IT, data teams, developers). Almost nobody speaks both languages fluently. When you leave this course, you will be able to sit in a meeting with a warehouse manager, understand what she actually needs, and translate that into a working application — without waiting for IT.

That skill is rare. Companies pay well for it.

### You will build things, not slides

By the end of Week 1, you will have a live web application deployed to the internet. Not a mockup. Not a slide deck. A real app, with real data, that anyone with a link can open. Each week, you add a new module. By the end of the semester, you have a complete supply chain management system in your portfolio.

When you interview for operations, analyst, or logistics roles, you can say: "Here is the URL. Let me show you what it does."

### AI does the coding. You provide the logic.

You do not need to know how to program. The assessable skill in this course is not Python syntax — it is **supply chain thinking**. You will learn to describe what a system needs to do in precise enough terms that an AI can build it. That is exactly what operations managers and analysts do when they work with software vendors and IT teams. You are learning that skill at a fraction of the cost.

### The tools are free and industry-standard

GitHub, Python, Streamlit — these are real tools used by real companies. When you graduate, your GitHub profile follows you. The projects you build here become evidence of capability, not just credentials.

### You will learn to think in systems

The deeper skill this course teaches is decomposing messy business problems into clean data structures. Maria at Peak Fuel Foods has a chicken shortage problem. Before you can solve it, you have to ask: what data would tell me there is a shortage? Where does that data come from? What other data does it connect to? That systems-thinking habit transfers to every role you will ever hold.

## The Mega Exam — Live Demo

The capstone assessment for this course is the Mega Exam. You will receive a dataset for a company you have never seen before. In a defined time window, you will build a working Streamlit app that answers specific operational questions about that company.

Today, we will walk through a completed example together so you know exactly what you are working toward from Day 1.

### What the demo covers

- The structure of the exam dataset (multiple CSVs, unknown schema)
- How to orient yourself quickly to a new company's data
- The kinds of questions the exam asks ("Does this company have enough inventory to fulfill next week's demand?")
- How a finished exam submission looks and functions

**The goal:** You will leave today knowing what the finish line looks like. Every lab exercise and homework assignment between now and the exam is a rehearsal for this.

### Key takeaway

The Mega Exam is not a test of memorization. It is a test of transfer — can you take the patterns you practiced on Peak Fuel Foods and apply them to a new context? If you build every module this semester and understand *why* each one works, the exam is straightforward.

## Team Formation

You will work as a team throughout the semester for Readiness Assurance Tests (RATs). Each team:

- Has 3–5 members
- Stays together for the full semester
- Completes iRATs individually and tRATs collaboratively

Teams are formed today. Your instructor will facilitate.

**What teams are good for in this course:** RATs reward teams where members have read the material and can debate the answers. A team where one person read and the others didn't is almost no better than one person. A team where everyone read and disagrees on the answer is how you learn.
